The Lore

Three basketball stories from the golden age of being decent.

Story One

I mastered the art of looking like a bigger threat than I actually was.

In warmups, I was elite. The jumper looked pure, the footwork looked serious, and for a few glorious minutes I absolutely carried myself like somebody who might drop 20. Then the game started and I settled into my true role: solid minutes, honest effort, and just enough production to keep the coaches believing there was maybe one more level in me.

I was not the guy with the mixtape. I was the guy clapping on defense, sprinting back hard, setting a screen with full conviction, and hoping that counted as "intangibles" when the stat sheet got a little quiet.

Story Two

My game was built on hustle, angles, and a dangerous amount of self-belief.

If there was a loose ball, I was diving. If there was a rebound to be tipped twice before somebody else actually secured it, that was my lane. I learned that being "mid" is not the same as being useless. There is real value in being the guy who talks, rotates, runs hard, and occasionally surprises everyone by making a shot at the exact moment it becomes funniest.

Some players were born scorers. I was more of a momentum intern. I helped the team vibe stay high, made the occasional smart play, and absolutely celebrated every bucket like it was going on the local sports report.

Story Three

The best part was never being a superstar.

High school basketball was fun because it gave me stories, teammates, bus rides, nerves before tipoff, and that weird confidence boost that comes from wearing a uniform. Being "mid" meant I got the full experience without the pressure of pretending I was about to get a scholarship offer at halftime.

I was good enough to care a lot, competitive enough to remember random games forever, and average enough to laugh about it now. Honestly, that is kind of the perfect lane.
